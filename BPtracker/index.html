HSE calls for calm as doctors raise fears of equipment shortage

Sunday, March 22

Hospital consultants reporting widespread shortage of protective masks and gowns. HSE says these are ‘wartime operations’ and promises that new stock will arrive.


The HSE has sought to play down mounting concerns about a lack of protective equipment for frontline health workers in the face of the starkest warning yet from hospital doctors.

The Coronavirus Ireland Podcast: The economic impact of Covid-19

Sunday, March 22

Nadine O‘Regan is joined by Business Post Markets Editor Ian Guider to discuss the impact that the coronavirus pandemic has had on Irish businesses and the economy at large as well as what it will take to get us all back on our feet.

126 new cases, bringing total to 683.

Friday, March 20

A further 126 cases of Covid-19 have been confirmed in the Republic, taking the total to 683.

The National Public Health Emergency Team said that the median age of the cases recorded up to Wednesday was 44 and that 32 per cent of all those who contracted the virus had received hospital treatment.



Tony Holohan, the chief medical officer, said:

“Now is not the time for complacency. Every citizen who is practising social distancing, who is taking precautions to limit the spread of this virus, is doing their country a service.”

Ryanair cut staff pay by 50 per cent

Friday, March 20

Ryanair announced it would cut staff and executive pay by 50 per cent as a result disruption to its business.
“We want to avoid mass lay-offs but the only way we can avoid mass lay-offs in our industry . . . is going to be payroll support for the next two to four months,” Michael O’Leary, Ryanair’s chief executive, told the Financial Times. Other businesses scaling down because of the pandemic include Ikea, which closed its British and Irish stores from 6pm today.

Europe the 'epicentre' of the outbreak

Friday, March 13

With the death toll in Italy surpassing 1,000 and cases being confirmed across the continent, the World Health Organisation declared that Europe was now the epicentre of the outbreak.

"Any countries that look at the experience of other countries with large epidemics and think ‘That won't happen to us’ is making a deadly mistake – it can happen to any country,” said Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, director general of the WHO.

'Unprecedented' Irish shutdown announced

Thursday, March 12

Speaking from Washington DC, Leo Varadkar announced an unprecedented shutdown of Irish schools, crèches, universities and public buildings for 17 days.

The sudden escalation in restrictive measures was prompted by the rise in confirmed cases from 43 to 70 overnight, amid growing concern about unexplained community transmission.

“I know that I am asking people to make enormous sacrifices. We’re doing it for each other,” Varadkar said. “Together, we can slow the virus in its tracks and push it back. Acting together, as one nation, we can save many lives. Our economy will suffer. It will bounce back.”
Restrictions on the number of people who should gather led to sporting organisations and entertainment venues effectively closing until the end of the month.

World Health Organisation declare a 'pandemic'.

Wednesday, March 11

The World Health Organisation described the global outbreak as a pandemic for the first time.

There were 118,000 cases, and more than 4,000 deaths, as the virus found a foothold on every continent except for Antarctica.

"We have never before seen a pandemic sparked by a coronavirus. And we have never before seen a pandemic that can be controlled at the same time," Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, the WHO‘s Director-General, said.
